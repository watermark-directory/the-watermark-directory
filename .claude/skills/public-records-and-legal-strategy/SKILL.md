# Public Records & Legal Strategy

Methodology for transparency litigation and records work. The author is not necessarily a lawyer; this skill supports drafting and strategy, not the practice of law. Verify posture with counsel and, where available, an independent legal-research check before relying on it.

## Drafting requests

- **Specific by default.** Write requests tight enough that a refusal looks unreasonable on its face. Key items to permit numbers, instrument names, date ranges, and the agency's own vocabulary.
- **Deliberately broad only where thesis-critical.** Reserve breadth for the few items the investigation actually turns on; make everything else narrow.
- **Structure for the responder.** Parts keyed to identifiers (A/B/C against permit numbers or record series) make denial harder and partial production cleaner.
- After a denial, **restructure rather than resend.** A revised request that adopts the agency's terminology and narrows scope converts a categorical denial into an itemized one — which is far more litigable.

## Reading agency responses

- **Silence after acknowledgment** past the jurisdiction's reasonableness window can constitute *constructive denial*. Track the clock from acknowledgment, not from the original request.
- **A "no records" certification** shifts the posture from a production fight to an *adequacy-of-certification* challenge. Rebut it in writing — do not ignore it and do not treat it as the end.
- **A categorical exclusion** (a statute that removes something from the *definition* of a public record) is different from a *pleadable exemption* (an affirmative defense the agency must raise). A complaint generally need not pre-address a categorical exclusion. Know which one you are facing.

## Sequencing remedies

- **Mandamus first, constitutional challenge second.** Establish standing and a clean factual predicate through the ordinary records remedy before mounting a constitutional attack on an exclusion. The documented denial is the predicate; preserve it cleanly.
- Identify the single cleanest denial in the record and treat it as the predicate instrument for any later challenge. Document it as such at the time it happens.

## Holding arguments in reserve

When a live proceeding (an oral hearing, a deposition) will reward an argument more than the written record will, hold it. Put into writing what you must to preserve it; save the rest for the room where a real-time response has strategic value.

## Retention schedules

When a request concerns whether records *should* exist, check the applicable records-retention schedule before accepting a "no records" answer. A record the entity was required to keep changes the meaning of its absence.
