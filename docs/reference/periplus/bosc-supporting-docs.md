# Project BOSC — Supporting documents

**Summary finding: Project BOSC is Google's Allen County, Ohio
hyperscale datacenter campus.** Confirmed 2026-04-15 through the
Project Dazzler (Tilted Gate LLC) permit dump — sibling entity in
the Michael Montfort portfolio, filed in parallel, whose Section 401
WQC application names the DC Project Lead with a `@google.com`
email address. The Montfort shell portfolio (Bistrozzi LLC + Bistrozzi
Addition LLC + Tilted Gate LLC + Magenta Capital LLC) is Google's
Ohio site-acquisition vehicle.

a material fraction of Google's global datacenter footprint.

### Significance

**Wilson Sonsini has been Google's primary outside corporate counsel
since before the 2004 IPO.** WSGR represented Google at IPO and has
had a continuous anchor-client relationship with Google through its
entire corporate life. A Delaware-shell site-acquisition portfolio
running through WSGR DC with signatory delegation to a long-tenured
Senior Counsel is the exact pattern you'd expect for a Google-
principal operation: the client uses its anchor firm for entity
formation + administration, then layers per-jurisdiction counsel
(Vorys/Ziance in Ohio regulatory, Kendall & Meyer in Ohio land
transactions) for local work.

### Practice-area mismatch is itself informative

Montfort's listed practice is **strictly employee benefits +
compensation** — nothing about real estate, data centers, or land
use. Yet he's the named Manager on multi-state Delaware shell LLCs
being used exclusively for datacenter land acquisition (Bistrozzi /
Tilted Gate / Magenta Capital in Ohio, plus user-identified entities
in AR and TX).

Three readings, ranked by plausibility:

1. **Routine entity-administration delegation (most likely).** Large
   WSGR clients with thousands of subsidiaries globally (Google
   foremost) often delegate routine signatory duties — SOS filings,
   permit applications as "Manager on behalf of the LLC" — to a
   specific Senior Counsel in the right office for organizational
   convenience, not specialty fit. The real operators + decision-
   makers are inside the client, not at the law firm. Montfort
   is DC-located, is Senior Counsel (non-equity), and signs the
   local filings that require a named individual.

2. **Compensation-vehicle structuring (possible but speculative).**
   If the shell LLCs have employee-equity components (uncommon for
   pure land-holding entities), an Employee Benefits specialist
   being nominal Manager could be deliberate tax structure. Would
   require seeing the operating agreements to confirm.

3. **Wrong LLC-chain layer (unlikely).** The SOS filings explicitly
   name Montfort as Manager of the operating land-acquisition shells,
   so this doesn't hold.

### Why the mismatch strengthens rather than weakens the Google inference

The opposite of what you might think. If WSGR were fronting the
transaction at a partner level with a real-estate partner as
signatory, it would suggest a standalone real-estate client (PE
fund, colo developer, REIT). The actual pattern — admin-level
delegation to a Senior Counsel whose public brand is entirely
unrelated to the transaction type — is exactly what you see when a
large anchor client wants filings made without drawing attention
to a specific practice group or partner. The signatory is chosen
for convenience, not substance.

This is consistent with:

- **Bistrozzi LLC's very generic name** (no Google trademark, no
  industry signal)
- **Delaware registered-agent-address-only mailing** (2801 Centerville
  Rd, Wilmington — no physical Bistrozzi presence)
- **Bistrozzi LLC tax address is PMB 811** and **Tilted Gate LLC is
  PMB 160** at the same Wilmington DE building — commercial
  mail-receiving service, not corporate offices
- **No WSGR branding on any Ohio-record filing** — Vorys and Kendall &
  Meyer appear; WSGR's role is entirely at the entity level, not the
  transaction level

### Material findings

**Cash acquisition.** No deed of trust or mortgage was recorded
alongside any of the five deeds. **Both Bistrozzi LLC and Bistrozzi
Addition LLC bought their tracts with cash**, not bank or private-
credit debt. This is consistent with hyperscaler balance-sheet
behavior — a colo developer, REIT, or PE fund would almost certainly
have recorded a deed of trust naming the lender. Eliminates
intermediary-developer interpretations and confirms Google is the
direct capital source.

**Five local farming-family prior owners, all bought out in 2025-2026.** Classic greenfield assembly pattern:

- **Neff Farms, Inc.** — largest single acquisition at $8.5M; tract
  spans the township line (Sugar Creek Sec 1 SW + American Sec 12 NW)

  Sec 12 SW, held across two Living Trusts (Keaton A + Kyle C) with
  four co-trustees. This is a family estate-planning structure; the

  market value with consideration masked, or a bargain sale

## Why this matters

A **2.5 MGD sanitary forcemain at $30M** for a single development is industrial-scale. For comparison, [Lima's entire treated-water system envelope is ~14 MGD](../siting/evaluations/demin-plant.yaml) — the BOSC sanitary capacity alone is ~18% of that. This is consistent with a hyperscale data-center campus or a similarly water-intensive industrial use, not housing or commercial development.

A 2.5 MGD sustained sanitary discharge profile (high-temperature reject,
see below) is in the right magnitude for **cooling-tower blowdown of a
~50-100MW IT-load datacenter campus** at typical concentration ratios.
That's a hyperscale-class single-site load.

## Industrial-load signal: BOSC discharge temperature

The 2025-07-29 commissioner minutes (page 4) describe BOSC's temporary
flow handling: **"0.13 MGD at 83 degrees Fahrenheit temporary until PS
and FM are complete"**. The temperature is the tell:

- Residential / commercial wastewater typically runs 60-70°F at the lift
  station.
- Industrial **cooling-tower blowdown and process-cooling reject**
  routinely runs 80-100°F.
- A datacenter using evaporative cooling at design conditions discharges
  at the upper end of that range.

83°F discharge into a sanitary system is not what 30 homes produces.
It's consistent with the cooling-reject side of an industrial campus
that hasn't yet hit its full operational load — the 0.13 MGD is a
fraction of the 2.5 MGD design capacity, but the temperature is already
in the industrial range.

## OEPA Cloudinary DAM URL pattern (mapped)

Ohio EPA hosts current draft NPDES permits and fact sheets on its
Cloudinary asset DAM at predictable URLs keyed by OEPA permit ID:

```
https://dam.assets.ohio.gov/image/upload/epa.ohio.gov/Portals/35/permits/
  DraftPN/{OEPA-permit-id}.pdf       — Public Notice cover page
  doc/{OEPA-permit-id}.pdf           — Full NPDES permit document
  doc/{OEPA-permit-id}.fs.pdf        — Fact Sheet
```

OEPA permit IDs follow the pattern `{N}P{LETTER}{NNNNN}` (the leading
digit + middle letter encode plant class; the 5-digit number is the
facility serial). Allen County's three POTWs are 2PH00006, 2PH00007,
and 2PK00002. Probing nearby IDs (`2PH00008`, `2PK00001`, etc.)
yields 404s — there's no archive of historical modifications;
the URL serves only the *current* draft/permit.

PTIs (Permits to Install) and DFFOs (Director's Final Findings &
Orders) are NOT on this DAM — they live in the eDocPub ASP.NET
viewstate-based portal that we've already noted resists scraping.

This pattern would make a clean weekly-refresh connector against a
known watchlist of OEPA permit IDs (one per facility we care about):
catch the next BOSC-related modification (e.g. when American Bath
gets a permit modification to accommodate BOSC's tributary flow,
which it doesn't yet — see below).

## Shawnee II Phase 2: state DFFO, not the 1996 federal decree

The Shawnee II Phase 2 fact sheet (2023) states the basis explicitly:
**"In accordance with DFFOs, the proposed work includes one new SBR
basin..."**. DFFO = Director's Final Findings & Orders, the OEPA's
state-administrative enforcement instrument. So Shawnee II Phase 2 is
a **separate state enforcement track**, parallel to but distinct from
the 1996 federal consent decree (which addressed both Shawnee No. 2
and American Bath under Judge Katz's continuing jurisdiction). The
state DFFO itself is not yet in our scrape; the Shawnee II PTI from
March 2024 (referenced in 2024-05-07 minutes) would be the underlying
permit document.

Pre-Phase-2 Shawnee II already had **24 MGD UV-only blended-effluent
capacity** for high-flow events (with a 1.2 MG equalization basin).
Phase 2 adds secondary treatment capacity (SBR basins, sludge
processing, holding basin), not raw hydraulic throughput.

## Regulatory baseline: 1996 federal consent decree on the BOSC corridor

The American Bath plant — receiving facility for BOSC's FM-1 — is on
**Bluelick Road in Bath Township**, the same trunk road as the BOSC
site at NE Bluelick & Cole. Both the American Bath plant and the
Shawnee No. 2 plant have been under a **federal Clean Water Act
consent decree since 1996** (United States and State of Ohio v. Board
of County Commissioners of Allen County, Ohio, N.D. Ohio, Judge David
A. Katz, DJ #90-5-1-1-3841).

Source PDF + extracted text in [source-docs/](source-docs/).

Key terms of the 1996 decree:

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Parties | US (EPA Region V) + Ohio EPA, v. Allen County Commissioners |
| Plants covered | American Bath (NPDES OH0023841 / OEPA 2PH00007*BD) + Shawnee No. 2 (NPDES OH0023850 / OEPA 2PK00002*CD) |
| Civil penalty | $100,000 ($90k US, $10k Ohio) |
| **American Bath interim loading limit** | **1.5 MGD** (effluent concentration limits at this design flow) |
| Shawnee No. 2 interim loading limit | 2.0 MGD |
| Compliance milestones | Sludge thickening project (Shawnee #2): construction by Jun 29, 1996, ops by Sep 29, 1996; American Bath improvements: construction by Jun 30, 1996, compliance by Sep 30, 1996 |
| Termination | Requires 12 months of compliance + Board certification + EPA/OEPA concurrence — **may still technically be in force** |

**Implication for BOSC:** Adding **2.5 MGD of BOSC industrial discharge**
to a plant whose 30-year regulatory baseline was **1.5 MGD interim
loading** is a material load change. The 2024 Shawnee II Phase 2 SECAP
PTI ($21.95M, raising avg to 3.0 MGD / peak to 12.60 MGD) at the
*Shawnee No. 2* plant — a sister facility under the same decree —
may be either a permit modification under the 1996 decree's
continuing-jurisdiction clause OR a successor consent action.
Worth pulling the Shawnee II PTI from OEPA + checking PACER for
post-1996 docket activity in the original case to determine which.

The fact that BOSC's FM-1 routes to American Bath (1.5 MGD historical
baseline) AND that the 16" FM-2 was added to City of Lima for the bulk
of BOSC flow is now better explained: American Bath could not absorb
the 2.5 MGD design without re-permitting the underlying consent decree,
so the bulk had to bypass it via FM-2 to Lima.

## Adjacent project: Shawnee II POTW Phase 2 (NOT a BOSC dependency)

The Shawnee II Publicly Owned Treatment Works ("Shawnee WWTP Phase 2 SECAP" in
the 2024-05-07 minutes) is a **separate $21.95M expansion** funded by an OWDA
loan, with an OEPA Permit-to-Install issued March 2024. Stated drivers
(per the Sanitary Engineering projects page):

- **Ohio EPA consent decree** to eliminate flow-equalization basin overflows
- Increase average daily flow rating to **3.0 MGD**
- Increase peak hydraulic flow capacity to **12.60 MGD**
- April 2024 → early 2027, ~$23M

**Geographically too far from BOSC** to be its receiver. Shawnee II is at
~40.659, -84.145 (Fort Shawnee), ~9 miles from the BOSC site. Both of
BOSC's forcemains (38,000 LF combined, ~3.4-3.8 mi each) terminate at
American Bath / American II / City of Lima — not at Shawnee II.

Shawnee II Phase 2 is parallel sanitary investment, not part of the BOSC
infrastructure stack. Worth tracking alongside BOSC because the county
is clearly in a multi-front capacity expansion across its sanitary system,
but it isn't BOSC-causing or BOSC-enabling.

## Where a demineralization plant would fit (working theory)

The two-forcemain BOSC discharge plan is a **cooling-side** infrastructure
package — it handles high-volume, high-temperature wastewater to standard
sanitary infrastructure. **Demin is the supply side**, not the discharge
side, and would be a *fourth* infrastructure project not yet in the
public record:

- A demin plant feeds **clean process water in** to the campus
- Its inputs come from a raw-water source (Ottawa River intake, dedicated
  wellfield, or a Lima municipal tap — see [demin-plant evaluation](../siting/evaluations/demin-plant.yaml) for the 4× Allen County sized model)
- Its **reject** (high-TDS brine, ~14.4 MGD at 4× county scale) is the
  load problem — too saline for sanitary, requires NPDES surface discharge
  to a major receiving water (Ottawa River with Q7Q10 dilution)

Distinguishing signals to watch for:

- **Raw-water intake permit** — Ohio Department of Natural Resources
  large-quantity withdrawal permit (groundwater) or NPDES intake permit
  (surface). Neither is in the public record yet for BOSC.
- **NPDES outfall** to Ottawa River for high-TDS brine reject —
  separate from the BOSC sanitary forcemains.
- **Raw-water transmission main** (48-60" class for ~58 MGD raw at 4× scale)
  from intake to BOSC site — would be a major easement filing.

If any of those surface, the demin theory is confirmed. If the only
infrastructure is the BOSC PS+FM and the Shawnee II POTW upgrade,
the load profile fits a cooling-only campus without on-site demin
(buying ultra-pure water from a third party or running smaller
in-line polishing).
